## Overview

The Caipora is a powerful forest spirit and guardian of wildlife from Brazilian folklore, with deep roots in Tupi-Guarani Indigenous mythology. The name derives from Tupi, meaning 'inhabitant of the forest' or 'dweller of the woods.' As a protector of small game and wild animals, the Caipora punishes hunters who violate rules of fair play — particularly those who hunt pregnant females, take more than they need, or hunt on Fridays, Sundays, and religious holidays. The entity has markedly different physical descriptions across regions: in some traditions it appears as a dark-skinned, hairy, dwarf-like Indigenous child (caboclinho or caboclinha), while in other regions it manifests as a giant hairy man. In Ceará it is described as bristly-headed with sharp teeth and ember-like eyes; in Bahia as small and almost black; in Ilhéus as fair-skinned. The Caipora rides peccaries, tapirs, or other forest animals as mounts, wields a stick or spiny vine whip, and uses fireflies as scouts. It carries a pipe — allegedly made from human bone — and can be bribed with tobacco and cachaça liquor. The creature can shapeshift and is said to be able to resurrect slain animals. It causes 'caipórismo' — a curse of perpetual bad luck — on hunters who violate its rules. The Caipora is often confused with the Curupira, another Brazilian forest guardian, but they are distinct entities with different characteristics and regional variations. Its role has evolved into a symbol in contemporary Brazilian environmental movements.
